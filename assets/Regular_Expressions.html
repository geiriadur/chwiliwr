Regular Expressions

 

 

Examples

 

1.      \bmarch\b  finds every example of the word march with word boundaries (\b) surrounding it. This is the most effective way to find whole words.
 

2.      \bm(a|ei)rch\b finds every example of march or meirch with word boundaries (\b) surrounding it.

Brackets ( a ) are used to group items; | means ‘or’.
 

3.      [mf](a|ei)rch finds examples of march or meirch together with the mutated forms, e.g.  march, farch, meirch and feirch. It also finds cyfarch, farchnad, marchog and orwyddfeirch because the word boundaries have not been set this time.

Square brackets [ and ] enclose lists of individual symbols, so [mf] means  m or f. (The order is not important.)

 

4.      \b(c|g|ngh|ch)effyl(au)?\b finds examples of ceffyl or ceffylau together with the mutated forms. This should also find ceffyl, geffyl, ngheffyl, cheffyl, ceffylau, geffylau, ngheffylau, and cheffylau.

 

5.      \bll[ae]dd(af|i|a|odd|ir|[ae]s*|wyd|id)\b finds examples of the verb lladd. This can be used as a pattern for verbs.

 

 

Every symbol in a regular expression represents itself apart from those listed below:

 

. [ ] { } ( ) * + ? | ^ $ \

 

.              represents any single letter/symbol yn cynrychioli unrhyw lythyren – including a space (except in lists of symbols).

[ ]           encloses a list of symbols, e.g. [abc] or a list of letters, e.g. [a-z], see ex. 3.

{ }           shows how many times an expression is repeated, e.g. a{3} matches aaa

( )           groups items, see ex. 2, above.

*             repeats the previous expression zero or more times, so .* represents anything or nothing.

+             repeats the previous expression one or more times, so .+arch represents march and farch, &c., but not arch.

?             makes the previous item optional, e.g. \b[mf]?arch\b represents march, farch and arch.

|             means ‘or’ when it separates items, e.g. (march|farch|marchog|farchog) means any of these words, but this could be written more concisely as [fm]arch(og)?.

^             indicates the beginning of a line.

at the beginning of a list between square brackets it means NOT anything in the list, e.g. \b[^fm]arch\b finds every word of 5 letters that ends in ‘-arch’, but NOT march or farch (it finds parch and barch).

$             indicates the end of a line. These last two are called anchors.

-              in a list shows a range, e.g. [a-z] means any lower-case letter, [A-Z] means any upper-case letter and [a-zA-Z] means any letter (lower- or upper-case), and [0-9] means any digit (the same as [0123456789]. (N.B. the order of letters is important: searching for [z-a] will fail.

\             is used before any one of the symbols above (and \ itself) as ‘escape’, meaning the symbol itself without its special function, i.e. \( means (, and \ can be indicated by \\.

 

 

Notes

 

Accents are important, i.e. tân must be used to find examples of the word tân, and tan to find tan; t[âa]n will find both.

 

6 = w  in some Middle Welsh texts

 

It’s better to be as open-ended as possible to start with and then restrict the search after seeing the search results.
